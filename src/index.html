Research

In all neurons, regulation of proteins through processing, localization, or degradation is critical to ensuring that cells can send and receive information efficiently and with high fidelity. One way for cells to tag proteins for different regulatory fates is through modification by the small protein ubiquitin. The importance of ubiquitin-mediated regulation in the nervous system is illustrated by the fact that mutation of specific ubiquitin ligases and deubiquitinating enzymes is implicated in neural diseases. My research focuses on the role of ubiquitin in the regulation of neurotransmitters in the model organism Caenorhabditis elegans, a nematode worm.

In C. elegans, much research has focused on understanding the role of ubiquitin at the synapses of interneurons, but less is known about how ubiquitin regulates proteins in sensory neurons. Research in my laboratory will provide insight into the cell biology of ubiquitin-based protein regulation in different types of neurons. Because many ubiquitin system proteins in C. elegans are similar or homologous to those in mammals, elucidating these pathways in the worm are likely to provide key insights into the functioning of sensory neurons in humans.

Teaching

My teaching philosophy is rooted in the principles of inquiry, discussion, and immersion, all of which are central to scientific understanding and progress. By engaging my students in the classroom and in the laboratory, I aim to help them acquire intellectual tools that will allow them to answer scientific and humanitarian questions.

I am an advocate for bringing undergraduate studenst into research settings. Three major types of undergraduate research - laboratory courses, individual research opportunities, and research positions - introduce students to the excitement of discovery and teach valuable research and reasoning skills.
